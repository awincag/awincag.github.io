Philosophy and Process

There are a few areas that drive my passion and approach to design.

"Simple ain't Easy"

Thelonious Monk

Designing simple and elegant solutions are 1) rarely discovered the first 25+ tries 2) not simple at all. I'm of the camp that in order to design a beautiful user experience and a great product, it's going to take a lot grinding and a whole lot of iteration (Joe Stewart of Work & Co, would call it 'brute force').

"Build around people problems, not company problems"

Nikel Blaase

Great design all starts with a great problem. If there's not a user problem, then there's probably not much, if any, design needed at all. Without a problem, you have a feature, not a digital product or service. A truly great product doesn't just provide more than just a fantastic flow, amicable animations and eye-candy branding (it definitely needs all of that), but it needs to provide value to the user, a lot of value. When you can find the value a product provides the user, that's when the product becomes more than just a feature or zombie app.

To solve people problems, that means understanding people and businesses, not just how to code. Being able to optimize a stylesheet is great for improving the 0.5 seconds of a persons life, but if that product doesn't solve their problem, you just wasted a lot more than 0.5 seconds. To this end, with all my projects, I truly push to find that deeper problem that people have and solve it, really, really well. It's not the "what lawnmower do I use?" It's the "what's the best way to key my grass short."

Design is not a manufactured good.

All too often, I hear people at work talk about how great their process is. How their process allows them to build the best products. I would disagree. Having a great process is only a fraction of the journey. Great design means bespoke solutions for each problem.

No two products are identical. Every solution is unique and every journey to find that solution is unique. While I have general practices that help solve problems, the only thing that truly stays the same in every project is finding out: What is that core problem?

From there, the journey can manifest itself in a variety of ways. Dependent on the amount of initial data, I can start with user interviews, core screens, design studio with the team or a varities of other activities that help drive towards that solution. Ultimately, my job as a designer is to help provide value to people through digital products & services.
